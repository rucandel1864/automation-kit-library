How to Write an AI Usage Policy for a Small Business

Small teams usually do not need a heavyweight governance program before they start using AI. They do need clear rules for what staff can paste into AI tools, what AI can draft, when a human must approve the output, and who owns review.

A useful first AI usage policy should be short enough for the team to read and specific enough to change behavior.

Start with allowed use cases

List the work where AI is allowed to help. Good early examples include summarizing internal notes, drafting first-pass SOPs, rewriting non-sensitive copy, creating meeting agendas, and organizing public information.

Define prohibited inputs

The policy should say what staff must not paste into AI tools. For most small businesses, that includes passwords, private customer data, employee records, financial account details, contracts, health information, unreleased business plans, and anything covered by a confidentiality duty.

Add human approval gates

AI can draft and structure work, but a person should approve customer-facing messages, legal or compliance language, financial decisions, hiring decisions, security actions, refunds, and anything that commits the business to a promise.

Name the owner

Assign one owner for policy updates and exception review. Without an owner, the policy becomes a document people forget after launch.

Use a first-version rollout

Do not wait for a perfect policy. Create version one, review it with the responsible business owner, share it with the team, and schedule a 30-day review after staff have used it in real workflows.

Simple policy structure

  • Purpose and scope.
  • Approved AI use cases.
  • Prohibited inputs.
  • Human approval requirements.
  • Tool and account rules.
  • Escalation and exception process.
  • Review owner and review date.
