AI usage policy template for small business teams

Use this framework when your team wants the productivity of AI-assisted workflows without turning every prompt into a compliance guessing game.

A good AI usage policy does not need to be long. It needs to tell people what they may use AI for, what they must never paste into AI tools, when human approval is required, and who owns the review process.

Copyable policy structure

# AI Usage Policy

Purpose:
Define safe, useful, human-reviewed AI use for internal business workflows.

Allowed use cases:
- Draft internal SOPs.
- Summarize meeting notes.
- Classify non-sensitive inbox messages.
- Draft lead follow-up for human review.

Prohibited inputs:
- Passwords, API keys, or private credentials.
- Customer payment details.
- Medical, legal, financial, or regulated personal information.
- Confidential contracts or unreleased financials.

Human approval required before:
- Sending external messages.
- Changing customer records.
- Publishing documents.
- Making commitments on behalf of the company.

Review cadence:
Review the policy monthly during rollout, then quarterly.

What to decide before rollout

  • Workflow scope: Start with low-risk admin workflows such as inbox triage, meeting summaries, lead follow-up drafts, and SOP documentation.
  • Input rules: Give operators a short list of prohibited inputs. If the list is vague, people will guess under time pressure.
  • Approval gates: Require human review before any external message, customer-record change, public document, or business commitment.
  • Ownership: Assign a policy owner, workflow owner, and escalation owner. AI adoption fails quietly when no one owns the rules.
  • Evidence: Keep approved prompts, SOPs, risk notes, and rollout decisions in one place so the process can be reviewed later.
